# Vaccination Demand and Barrier Guidance

## WHO BeSD Framework

The WHO Behavioural and Social Drivers of Vaccination framework supports programmes to understand reasons for low vaccine uptake and use evidence to design and evaluate tailored interventions.

The main domains are:

### Thinking and Feeling

Examples include confidence in vaccine benefits, concerns about safety, perceived disease risk, and trust.

### Social Processes

Examples include family and community norms, recommendations from health workers, trusted leaders, and social support.

### Motivation

Examples include intention, willingness, and readiness to vaccinate.

### Practical Issues

Examples include distance, cost, service hours, availability, transport, reminders, and ease of access.

## Tailoring Principle

Interventions should respond to locally observed barriers. A general communication campaign is not automatically appropriate for an access or service-availability problem.

Examples:

- Knowledge gap: clear factual explanation and trusted interpersonal communication.
- Social influence: engagement with trusted community, religious, cultural, or local leaders.
- Missed reminder: reminder and defaulter follow-up mechanisms.
- Distance or timing: outreach, adjusted service hours, or better service information.
- Negative service experience: service-quality improvement and health-worker support.
- Safety concern: factual, empathetic discussion and referral to current official guidance.

These are planning categories, not automatic prescriptions. Local programme teams must validate the barrier and approve the intervention.

## Uganda Communication Principles

Uganda's National Immunisation Strategy calls for communication that is:

- Informative
- Timely
- Precise
- Comprehensible
- Age-specific
- Geographically targeted
- Appropriate to the intended audience and channel

The strategy also recognises the need to research, pretest, and approve immunisation messages.
