# Uganda Routine Immunization — VHT Talking Points & Radio Scripts
### English / Luganda
**Source base:** Ministry of Health / UNICEF Uganda *Question and Answer Booklet on Routine Immunisation*, MOH Health Promotion and Education Division. Toll-free line: **0800 100 066**.

> **DEMONSTRATION DRAFT — NOT APPROVED FOR PUBLICATION OR BROADCAST.**
>
> This file is retained as a source input for the Equitable Immunisation Monitoring prototype. It must not be represented as Ministry of Health-approved content. Schedule statements require reconciliation with the Ministry of Health Routine Immunization Schedule dated 6 November 2025, and all Luganda text requires review by a qualified native speaker and the appropriate Uganda immunisation communication authority.
>
> Known conflicts to resolve before use:
> - The current schedule image includes additional malaria vaccine contacts at 6, 7, 8, and 18 months, so “five visits before the first birthday” is not a complete description of the current schedule.
> - The current schedule image shows one HPV dose for 10-year-old girls; the two-dose statement below is outdated.
> - Attribution such as “This message is brought to you by the Ministry of Health” cannot appear in generated demonstration materials without explicit approval.

> ⚠️ **Translation note:** The Luganda text below is a working draft, not an MOH-vetted translation. Disease names, dosage terms, and idiom can carry real weight in community health messaging — please have it reviewed by a Luganda-speaking MOH Health Promotion/UNEPI officer (or a district Health Educator) before printing or broadcasting. Flag anything that sounds stiff or overly literal; I've aimed for natural spoken register but I'm not a substitute for a native reviewer here.

## 1. VHT Household Talking Points

Use as a checklist for a home visit or doorstep conversation — not a script to read word-for-word.

| # | English | Luganda (draft) |
|---|---------|------------------|
| 1 | Good day. I'm [Name], your Village Health Team member. I've come to talk about your child's immunization. | Gyebale ko. Nze [Erinnya], Omukulembeze w'Ekibiina ky'Abakulembeze b'Ebyobulamu (VHT) mu kitundu kino. Nzize okwogera naawe ku by'okugema omwana wo. |
| 2 | Immunization protects your child from more than 10 dangerous diseases and helps them grow up strong and healthy. | Okugema kukuuma omwana wo okuva ku ndwadde ezisukka mu kkumi ez'akabi, era kuyamba omwana okukula ng'ali mu bulamu obulungi. |
| 3 | Before their first birthday, your child needs 5 visits — at birth, 6 weeks, 10 weeks, 14 weeks, and 9 months. Each one protects against different diseases. | Nga omwana tannaba kutuuka mu mwaka gumu, alina okugenda emirundi etaano — nga azaalibwa, mu wiiki 6, mu wiiki 10, mu wiiki 14, ne mu myezi 9. Buli lwendagalo lukuuma ku ndwadde ez'enjawulo. |
| 4 | These vaccines are completely free and approved as safe by the Ministry of Health, WHO, and UNICEF. | Empeera zino za bwereere era zikakasiddwa okuba ez'obukuumi Ministry of Health, WHO, ne UNICEF. |
| 5 | Getting several vaccines at one visit will not overwhelm your child's body — it is safe. | Okufuna empeera nnyingi ku lugendo lumu tekyusa ku bulamu bw'omwana — kirungi era bulungi. |
| 6 | Vaccines do not cause infertility — that is not true. | Empeera tezireetera muntu bukka — ekyo si kya mazima. |
| 7 | A little fever or swelling where the injection was given is normal. It goes away on its own — no need for medicine. | Okufuna namaanyi ga mubiri oba okuzimba awakubiddwa essirika kya bulijjo. Kigenda kyekka — teweetaaga ddagala. |
| 8 | If you have a daughter who has turned 10, she should get the HPV vaccine — 2 doses, 6 months apart — to protect her from cervical cancer later in life. | Bw'oba olina omuwala aweza emyaka 10, alina okufuna empeera ya HPV — endagalo bbiri, buli emu nga wakati waazo emyezi mukaaga — okumukuuma ku kookolo w'ensingo mu biseera eby'omu maaso. |
| 9 | Women aged 15 to 49 should also be vaccinated against tetanus — it protects you and any future children. | Abakyala abali wakati w'emyaka 15 ne 49 nabo balina okufuna empeera ey'ekikonko (tetanus) — ekuuma ggwe n'abaana bo abagenda okuzaalibwa mu biseera eby'omu maaso. |
| 10 | Please bring your child to [facility/outreach site] on [date] to continue or complete their immunization. | Nkusaba oleete omwana wo e [ekifo/wooteka] ku [olunaku] okwongera oba okumaliriza okugema kwe. |
| 11 | If you have questions, call the free Ministry of Health line: 0800 100 066. | Bw'oba n'ebibuuzo, kuba essimu eya bwereere eya Ministry of Health: 0800 100 066. |

**Tips for delivery:**
- Lead with the schedule and the free/safe message — those two points answer most hesitancy on their own.
- Save the myth-busting lines (rows 5–7) for when a caregiver raises a specific worry, rather than front-loading them (introducing a myth to rebut it can sometimes reinforce it).
- End every conversation with a concrete next step: a date, a place, a name.

## 2. Radio Spot — 60 Seconds

### English

> **[SFX: gentle background music, community sounds]**
>
> **NARRATOR:** Every parent wants their child to grow up strong and healthy.
>
> **MOTHER (warm voice):** My child has been immunized five times before her first birthday — at birth, six weeks, ten weeks, fourteen weeks, and nine months. Each visit protected her against a different disease.
>
> **NARRATOR:** Immunization protects children from more than ten dangerous diseases — including measles, whooping cough, polio, and tuberculosis. It is completely free at all government health facilities and outreach sites — and it is approved safe by the Ministry of Health, WHO, and UNICEF.
>
> **MOTHER:** Some people worry that many vaccines at once are too much for a child. That is not true — it is safe. Others worry vaccines cause infertility. That is also not true.
>
> **NARRATOR:** Protect your child. Complete the full immunization schedule. Visit your nearest health facility or ask your Village Health Team.
>
> For more information, call the Ministry of Health toll-free line: zero-eight-hundred, one-hundred, triple-zero-six-six.
>
> This message is brought to you by the Ministry of Health.

>
> Okufuna amawulire amalala, kuba essimu eya bwereere eya Ministry of Health: zero-eight-hundred, one-hundred, triple-zero-six-six (0800 100 066).
>

## 3. Radio Spot — 30 Seconds

### English

> **NARRATOR:** Your child needs 5 immunization visits before their first birthday — at birth, 6 weeks, 10 weeks, 14 weeks, and 9 months. Free. Safe. Approved by the Ministry of Health, WHO, and UNICEF.
>
> Multiple vaccines at once will not harm your child. Vaccines do not cause infertility.
>
> Visit your nearest health facility or ask your Village Health Team. For information, call 0800 100 066.
>
> A message from the Ministry of Health.

### Luganda (draft)

> **OMWANNYONYOLA:** Omwana wo yeetaaga okugendayo emirundi etaano okumugemya nga tannaba kutuuka mu mwaka gumu — nga azaalibwa, mu wiiki mukaaga, mu wiiki kkumi, mu wiiki kkumi n'ennya, ne mu myezi mwenda. Kya bwereere. Kirungi. Kikakasiddwa Ministry of Health, WHO, ne UNICEF.
>
> Empeera nnyingi ku mulundi gumu tezikosa mwana wo. Empeera tezireetera bukka.
>
> Genda ku wooteka w'ebyobulamu okuli okumpi, oba obuuze Omukulembeze wo owa VHT. Okufuna amawulire, kuba essimu 0800 100 066.
>
> Obubaka buno buva ku Ministry of Health.

## Notes for use

- **Numbers stay in English/numerals** in most Luganda radio delivery (dates, the phone number) — that's standard practice in Ugandan health broadcasting since digits are read numerically either way.
- If this is for a specific district or campaign (e.g., HPV catch-up, measles outbreak response, malaria vaccine rollout), the schedule/disease lines can be swapped out while keeping the structure and safety/myth-busting lines intact.
- Before recording or printing, route the Luganda text through UNEPI's Advocacy and Risk Communication desk or a district Health Educator — they'll have the current locally-approved terminology for specific vaccines (e.g., how PCV, rotavirus, and HPV are conventionally referred to in Luganda-language IEC materials).
